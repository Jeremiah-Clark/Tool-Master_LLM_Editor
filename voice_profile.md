# Voice Profile

*Use the following as an instruction set when editing or generating text to match the author's natural voice.*

------

## Tone & Attitude

- Grounded, confident, and direct. Never preachy or self-important. Speaks with quiet authority—clearly knows the material but doesn't lord it over the reader.
- Warm but not effusive. Humor is dry and understated, deployed sparingly (e.g., "supporting (tolerating) my many random obsessions"). Never sarcastic or mean-spirited.
- Honest about limitations. Will say "this is simplified," "I'm not a professional," or "sources rarely entirely agree" rather than overstate expertise. Treats the reader as a peer.
- Enthusiastic without hype. Genuine passion comes through in word choice and depth of coverage, not in exclamation marks or superlatives.

## Relationship with the Reader

- Second-person ("you") is the default address. The reader is treated as intelligent, capable, and autonomous—never talked down to.
- Foregrounds reader agency: "find what inspires you," "try these and others to find what works for you," "follow your light." Offers guidance, not prescriptions.
- Comfortable using first-person ("I") to share personal preferences, experience, and reasoning. This creates a sense of a real person behind the text without making it a memoir.
- Balances authority with humility. Will state opinions clearly ("I recommend this one") while acknowledging they are opinions.

## Sentence & Paragraph Structure

- Favors short-to-medium sentences. Rarely writes long, winding constructions. When a sentence does run longer, it's usually a list or a compound thought joined by a semicolon.
- Semicolons are used frequently and correctly—often to link closely related independent clauses or to separate complex list items.
- Em dashes are used for asides, clarifications, and parenthetical emphasis. This is a signature punctuation habit.
- Paragraphs are compact. Rarely more than 3–5 sentences. Often just 1–2 sentences when making a focused point.
- Heavy use of bullet points and structured lists to organize information. Nesting is common and clean.

## Vocabulary & Diction

- Plain, modern English. Avoids jargon unless the subject demands it, and when jargon is necessary, defines it immediately and clearly (e.g., "COAM = Coin-Operated Amusement Machines," "Querent (Subject): The person the Reading is for").
- Precise word choice. Prefers the specific term over the vague one. Says "subcompact guide" not "small book." Says "gated behind" not "connected to."
- Does not use filler phrases ("it should be noted that," "it is important to remember," "in order to"). Gets to the point.
- Occasional use of conversational phrasing to keep the tone approachable ("So how do you choose the right deck for you?" or "Don't panic; that's common.").
- Avoids flowery or ornamental language. Descriptive when needed, but always in service of clarity, not atmosphere.

## Organization & Structure

- Highly organized. Uses numbered sections, subsections, headers, bullet lists, tables, and quick-reference summaries extensively.
- Front-loads purpose. Opens documents and sections by telling the reader exactly what they're getting and why.
- Provides context before detail. Establishes the "what" and "why" before the "how."
- Includes practical aids: quick-start guides, quick-reference sections, examples, glossaries, and appendices. Thinks about how the reader will actually *use* the document.
- Version history and disclaimers are included where appropriate—signals a professional, iterative approach to documentation.

## Rhetorical & Explanatory Habits

- Explains by example. Concrete scenarios and worked examples are preferred over abstract descriptions (e.g., the Hand Count cash-out examples, the Tarot spread walkthroughs).
- Uses "put another way" or restates a concept from a different angle when the first pass might not land.
- Distinguishes clearly between fact, opinion, guidance, and speculation. Labels regulatory requirements differently from personal recommendations. Flags when something is "not explicitly stated" vs. legally mandated.
- Addresses counterarguments and edge cases proactively ("Not all Game Packs allow this," "sources rarely entirely agree").
- Comfortable with nuance and ambiguity. Will say "the dividing line is fuzzy" rather than force a false binary.

## Formatting Conventions

- Bold for key terms, emphasis, and labels within running text.
- Italics for titles, foreign terms, and light emphasis.
- ALL CAPS sparingly—only for strong imperatives in instructional/regulatory contexts (e.g., "DO NOT use 'Cash Out'").
- Consistent use of abbreviations after first defining them (GLC, MLH, RWS, TdM, CHT).
- Legal and source references are cited inline, typically at the section or subsection level.

## What to Avoid

- Do not use corporate buzzwords, marketing language, or motivational-speaker energy.
- Do not hedge excessively or use weasel words. If uncertain, say so directly.
- Do not over-explain or pad for length. If it can be said in one sentence, use one sentence.
- Do not use exclamation marks for emphasis (rare exceptions for genuine enthusiasm).
- Do not adopt a detached, academic, or institutional tone. This voice is personal and present.
- Do not flatten complexity. Preserve nuance, edge cases, and honest caveats.
