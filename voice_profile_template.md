# Voice Profile Template

*Define your writing voice. This template helps you articulate the tone, personality, and style you want to maintain across your work.*

------

## What Is a Voice Profile?

A voice profile is a guide to your natural writing voice—the personality, tone, and rhetorical habits that make your writing recognizably *yours*. It answers questions like:

- What mood or attitude do you convey?
- How do you address your reader?
- What punctuation and sentence structures do you favor?
- What words and phrases feel "you" and which ones don't?

A voice profile becomes especially useful when you're editing your own work or working with an AI editor. Instead of starting from scratch each time, you can reference it to stay consistent.

**This template has 8 core sections.** Fill in each one with 2–4 sentences or bullets describing your actual habits. Be honest—write what you *actually* do, not what you think you *should* do.

## WORKED EXAMPLE: "Grounded Guide"

This example shows what a filled-in voice profile looks like. Use it as a reference while completing the template above.

---

### Tone & Attitude

Grounded, confident, and direct. Never preachy or self-important. Speaks with quiet authority—clearly knows the material but doesn't lord it over the reader. Warm but not effusive. Humor is dry and understated, deployed sparingly. Never sarcastic or mean-spirited. Honest about limitations. Enthusiastic without hype. Genuine passion comes through in word choice and depth of coverage, not in exclamation marks or superlatives.

### Relationship with the Reader

Second-person ("you") is the default address. The reader is treated as intelligent, capable, and autonomous—never talked down to. Foregrounds reader agency: "find what inspires you," "try these and others to find what works for you." Uses first-person ("I") to share personal preferences, experience, and reasoning. This creates a sense of a real person behind the text without making it a memoir. Balances authority with humility. Will state opinions clearly ("I recommend this one") while acknowledging they are opinions.

### Sentence & Paragraph Structure

Favors short-to-medium sentences. Rarely writes long, winding constructions. When a sentence does run longer, it's usually a list or a compound thought. Em dashes are used frequently for asides, clarifications, and parenthetical emphasis. Paragraphs are compact—rarely more than 3–5 sentences, often just 1–2 sentences when making a focused point. Heavy use of bullet points and structured lists to organize information. Nesting is common and clean.

### Vocabulary & Diction

Plain, modern English. Avoids jargon unless the subject demands it; when jargon is necessary, defines it immediately and clearly. Precise word choice. Prefers the specific term over the vague one. Does not use filler phrases ("it should be noted that," "in order to," "it is important to remember"). Occasional use of conversational phrasing to keep the tone approachable. Avoids flowery or ornamental language. Descriptive when needed, but always in service of clarity, not atmosphere.

### Organization & Structure

Highly organized. Uses numbered sections, subsections, headers, bullet lists, tables, and quick-reference summaries extensively. Front-loads purpose—opens documents and sections by telling the reader exactly what they're getting and why. Provides context before detail. Establishes the "what" and "why" before the "how." Includes practical aids: quick-start guides, quick-reference sections, examples, glossaries, and appendices.

### Rhetorical & Explanatory Habits

Explains by example. Concrete scenarios and worked examples are preferred over abstract descriptions. Uses "put another way" or restates a concept from a different angle when the first pass might not land. Distinguishes clearly between fact, opinion, guidance, and speculation. Addresses counterarguments and edge cases proactively. Comfortable with nuance and ambiguity. Will say "the dividing line is fuzzy" rather than force a false binary.

### Formatting Conventions

Bold for key terms, emphasis, and labels within running text. Italics for titles, foreign terms, and light emphasis. ALL CAPS very sparingly—only for strong imperatives in instructional or regulatory contexts. Consistent use of abbreviations after first defining them. Legal and source references are cited inline, typically at the section or subsection level.

### What to Avoid

Do not use corporate buzzwords, marketing language, or motivational-speaker energy. Do not hedge excessively or use weasel words. Do not over-explain or pad for length. Do not use exclamation marks for emphasis. Do not adopt a detached, academic, or institutional tone. Do not flatten complexity. Preserve nuance, edge cases, and honest caveats. Avoid the passive voice when it hides the actor ("Mistakes were made" instead of "I made a mistake").

## Notes for Users

- **You don't need to match this example.** The "Grounded Guide" is one voice among many. Your voice might be more formal, more playful, more minimalist, or more narrative—whatever is authentically yours.
- **Iterate:** You may not have a complete voice profile on day one. As you work, refine it. The template is a living document.
- **Use it as a reference:** Once you've filled in your profile, you can share it with an AI editor or collaborator so they understand your preferences upfront.
- **Consistency across work:** Your voice profile applies across all your writing—blogs, essays, social posts, emails—though the intensity or formality may shift by context.
