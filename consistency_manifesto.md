# Consistency & Stability Manifesto

*Foundational consistency principles that apply to all writing.*

------

## 1. The Canonical Term Map

- **Synonym Audit:** Identify if you're using multiple names for the same thing (e.g., "user," "customer," "subscriber").
- **Action:** Select one canonical term and use it consistently. Flag synonyms for replacement.
- **Avoid Near-Synonyms:** Don't use "user" and "client" interchangeably unless the distinction is intentional and defined.

## 2. Structural Parallelism

- **List Consistency:** If one item in a list starts with a verb (e.g., "Click the button"), all items should start with a verb.
- **Heading Hierarchy:** Headings at the same level should use consistent capitalization, grammatical structure, and tone.

## 3. Universal Anchor (Applies to ALL Writing)

These principles apply regardless of document family, mode, or voice profile:

### 1. Purpose First (The "What am I reading?" rule)

The opening of your document or section must answer, in plain language:
- What this is
- Who it is for
- What the reader can do with it

If the opening is atmospheric or narrative, include a grounding line (1–2 sentences) immediately after to establish purpose.

### 2. Clarity Beats Cleverness

- Prefer plain, modern English over elevated or ornate phrasing.
- If a sentence can be misread, rewrite it. Do not rely on tone or implication to carry meaning.
- Eliminate filler and throat-clearing: "In order to," "It should be noted," "Basically," "Very," "Really," "Just."

### 3. Consistency of Terms (No Term Drift)

- Use one canonical term per concept.
- If a term is defined, branded, or technical (e.g., a legal term, product name, feature name), reuse it exactly.
- Avoid near-synonyms that create ambiguity unless the distinction is intentional and defined.

### 4. Structure That Scans

- Default to short paragraphs.
- Prefer lists, tables, and headings over dense blocks of text.
- Flag "wall of text" paragraphs (over ~5 lines) and break them into separate paragraphs or lists.

### 5. Evidence vs. Inference (Label the Epistemics)

Clearly distinguish:
- What the text states (fact within the document)
- What you are inferring (interpretation)
- What you are suggesting (proposed change)

Any rewrite that changes meaning must be tagged as a suggestion and accompanied by a rationale.

### 6. Confidence Without Hype

- Avoid marketing fluff and corporate-speak.
- Keep a grounded, direct tone.
- Replace passive voice when it hides the actor ("Mistakes were made") with clear responsibility ("I made a mistake," "The system returned an error") unless the actor is genuinely unknown.
- If unsure whether passive voice is appropriate, flag it for review.

### 7. Reader Agency

- Treat the reader as capable. No condescension or over-explanation.
- When there are legitimate tradeoffs or choices, surface them explicitly and let the reader decide.

### 8. Mechanical Consistency

- Use US English punctuation and conventions.
- Use Oxford commas in lists.
- Headings follow consistent capitalization rules (Title Case unless otherwise specified in your voice profile).

---

## 4. Notes on Implementation

- **Universal Anchor takes priority.** If a family-specific logic module conflicts with one of the eight Anchor principles, follow the Anchor item.
- **Refer to your voice profile for tone.** The Anchor is about clarity and structure, not tone. Your voice profile governs tone and personality.
